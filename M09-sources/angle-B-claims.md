## Thesis

The danger isn't that AI gets things wrong — it's that it gets things wrong with certainty. SOPs must be redesigned around AI overconfidence, not just AI errors.

---

## Three Supporting Points

**Point 1 — What the source says and what it gets right:**
Bhatnagar's core finding is that confidence is the mechanism that turns hallucinations into exploits. A wrong answer delivered hesitantly gets scrutinized. The same wrong answer delivered confidently gets acted on. The paper calls confidence itself an "attack surface" and demonstrates through the automation bias loop how repeated confident AI outputs gradually erode human skepticism, even in skilled analysts.

Key quotes:
- *"Confidence itself has become an attack surface."* (p.18)
- *"A hallucinated output delivered hesitantly may still trigger scrutiny. The same output delivered confidently is more likely to be accepted, acted upon, and propagated."* (p.4–5)
- *"Confident recommendations compress uncertainty into seemingly decisive conclusions."* (p.4)

**Point 2 — Where you extend the argument (SOP angle):**
The paper identifies the problem as systemic but stays at the organizational/governance level. The SOP angle is the procedural gap it gestures at but doesn't name directly. Current SOPs were written for human judgment, not for AI confidence. They don't include steps like "verify before acting on AI output" because that wasn't needed before.

Key quote:
- *"As organizations integrate AI into standard operating procedures, recommendations generated by these systems begin to carry implicit authority."* (p.4)

**Point 3 — What this means for the future of SOPs:**
SOPs need a new design primitive: a **skepticism checkpoint** — a required pause-and-verify step any time an AI recommendation is the basis for a consequential action. Not a governance policy. A procedural step, written into the SOP, that counteracts automation bias by making skepticism mandatory rather than optional.

---

## Practical Takeaway

If you're a manager or process owner and your SOPs reference or assume AI tools, ask one question: *where in this procedure does a worker verify the AI before acting on it?* If there's no answer, that's the gap.
