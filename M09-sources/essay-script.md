# M09 Video Essay — Full Script

**Angle:** B — AI overconfidence as the mechanism; SOPs as the fix
**Target length:** ~750 words / 5 minutes
**Draft date:** 2026-03-13

---

## OPENING (~45 sec)

For the past few years, the conversation about AI risk has centered on one question: what happens when AI gets it wrong?

Wrong answers. Wrong data. Hallucinated citations. We've built workflows to catch mistakes — we fact-check, we double-check, we tell people to verify before they trust.

But there's a problem with that approach: it assumes we'll *know* when to verify.

What if the AI sounds completely certain? What if it doesn't hedge, doesn't qualify, doesn't say "I'm not sure"? What if it's wrong — and it doesn't look wrong?

That's the argument at the center of a 2025 paper by Pranshu Bhatnagar titled *Hallucination-Driven Exploits: Weaponizing AI False Confidence in Cybersecurity Systems*. Bhatnagar's core claim is direct: confident AI errors are categorically more dangerous than uncertain ones. And his conclusion is one worth sitting with: "Confidence itself has become an attack surface."

My thesis today: the danger isn't that AI gets things wrong. It's that it gets things wrong *with certainty*. And that means our SOPs — the procedures that govern how work actually gets done — need to be redesigned.

---

## POINT 1 — The confidence mechanism (~60 sec)

Here's the mechanism Bhatnagar describes.

When an AI output is hesitant — "I think this might be..." — humans engage their critical faculties. They slow down. They check. But when the same output is delivered confidently — no hedging, no qualifiers, stated as fact — something different happens. The output gets accepted, acted on, and passed along.

Bhatnagar calls this the automation bias loop. Each time a confident AI output turns out to be right, it reinforces trust. Over time, even skilled analysts stop questioning — not because they're careless, but because the pattern has trained them not to be skeptical.

This isn't just one researcher's observation. NIST's AI Risk Management Framework — specifically the Generative AI profile, published in 2024 — defines confabulation, their term for hallucination, as "the production of confidently stated but erroneous or false content... by which users may be misled or deceived." And NIST goes further: "Risks from confabulations may arise when users believe false content — often due to the confident nature of the response — leading users to act upon or promote the false information."

Two independent sources, same finding: confidence is the delivery mechanism that makes AI errors dangerous.

---

## POINT 2 — Where this lives: SOPs (~60 sec)

So where does this problem actually live inside an organization?

It lives in the procedures.

Bhatnagar writes: "As organizations integrate AI into standard operating procedures, recommendations generated by these systems begin to carry implicit authority." That phrase — *implicit authority* — is the key. When AI is embedded in a workflow, workers don't evaluate its outputs the way they'd evaluate a suggestion from a colleague. They treat it like a system output. And system outputs, by default, get followed.

NIST AI 600-1 names this directly: automation bias is "excessive deference to automated systems" that "can exacerbate other risks of GAI, such as risks of confabulation."

And NIST's governance framework says organizations must integrate AI trustworthiness characteristics into their "policies, processes, procedures, and practices."

That's exactly right. Procedures are where this has to be fixed. But here's the gap: NIST tells you that procedures need to change. It doesn't tell you what step to write.

---

## POINT 3 — The fix: a skepticism checkpoint (~60 sec)

That gap is what I want to name directly.

Current SOPs were written for human judgment. They assume the person executing the procedure is the one evaluating quality. When you add AI to a workflow, that assumption breaks — because the AI delivers outputs with a confidence that bypasses the evaluation step entirely.

The fix isn't better AI. A more accurate model still delivers outputs confidently. The fix is a redesigned SOP.

Specifically: a **skepticism checkpoint**. A required, written step in the procedure that says — before acting on an AI recommendation in a consequential decision, stop and verify.

Not a policy. Not a training. A *step*. Written into the procedure. Because if verification is optional, automation bias will make it optional in the wrong moments — the moments when it matters most.

This isn't about distrust. It's about procedure design that accounts for how humans actually respond to confident systems.

---

## TAKEAWAY + CLOSE (~45 sec)

Here's a practical test. If you're a manager or process owner and your SOPs reference AI tools — or your team uses them routinely — ask one question: *Where in this procedure does a worker verify the AI before acting on it?*

If there's no answer, that's the gap.

The research is clear. The standards agree. Confident AI errors are different from uncertain ones — they bypass the skepticism that catches mistakes. And right now, most procedures aren't designed to put that skepticism back in.

The danger isn't that AI gets things wrong. It's that it gets things wrong with certainty — and our procedures haven't caught up.
